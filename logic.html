Mathematical Logic

Mathematical logic is the set of logical theories elaborated in the course of the 19th century with the aid of an artificial notation and a rigorously deductive method.It serves as the basis on which we develop the rest of mathematics

Mathematical logic reflects the confluence of two traditions:

     I.  Formal philosophical logic
     II. Mathematics

Since its inception, mathematical logic has both contributed to and has been motivated by the study of foundations of mathematics.

Validity Checker

There are some rules we take as axioms called laws

In analyzing deductive arguments and the statements that occur in them it is helpful to be familiar with a number of equivalences that come up often.

DeMorgan's laws:

  • \(\lnot\)(P \(\land\) Q) is equivalent to (\(\lnot\)P) \(\lor\) (\(\lnot\)Q)
  • \(\lnot\)(P \(\lor\) Q) is equivalent to (\(\lnot\)P) \(\land\) (\(\lnot\)Q)

Commutative laws:

  • P \(\land\) Q is equivalent to Q \(\land\) P
  • P \(\lor\) Q is equivalent to Q \(\lor\) P

Associative laws:

  • P \(\land\) (Q \(\land\) R) is equivalent to (P \(\land\) Q) \(\land\) R
  • P \(\lor\) (Q \(\lor\) R) is equivalent to (P \(\lor\) Q) \(\lor\) R.

Idempotent laws:

  • P \(\land\) P is equivalent to P
  • P \(\lor\) P is equivalent to P

Distributive laws:

  • P \(\land\) (Q \(\lor\) R) is equivalent to (P \(\land\) Q) \(\lor\) (P \(\land\) R)
  • P \(\lor\) (Q \(\land\) R) is equivalent to (P \(\lor\) Q) \(\land\) (P \(\lor\) R)

Absorption laws:

  • P \(\lor\) (P \(\land\) Q) is equivalent to P
  • P \(\land\) (P \(\lor\) Q) is equivalent to P

Double Negation law:

  • \(\lnot\)\(\lnot\)P is equivalent to P
Formulas that are always true, such as P \(\lor\) (\(\lnot\)P), are called Tautologies
Similarly, formulas that are always false are called Contradictions

Predicate Logic

Predicate logic uses quantified variables over non-logical objects, and allows the use of sentences(propositions) that contain variables.

This distinguishes it from propositional logic, which does not use quantifiers or relations. In this sense, propositional logic is the foundation of predicate logic.

  • P : Socrates is a man
  • \(P(x)\) : x is a man
  • There exists x such that \(P(x)\)
  • (that x is socrates)

Variables

  • In mathematical reasoning it is often necessary to make statements about objects that are represented by letters called variables.
  • We have used a single letter such as P to represent these statements.Now we alter the notation slightly and write \(P(x)\), to stress that this is a statement about x.
  • The latter notation makes it easy to talk about substituting some value for x in the statement.

Quantifers

In predicate logic, A quantifier is an operator that specifies how many individuals in the domain of discourse satisfy the given formula.

A domain of discourse is the the collection of entities over which our variable may range

The two Quantifiers most used are :

  1.     I.  Universal Quantifier : \(\forall\)
  2.     II. Existential Quantifier : \(\exists\)

\(\forall x P(x)\)

\(\exists x P(x)\)

2. Order of operators matter:

  • I.  \(\exists y \forall x(x + y = 5)\)
  • II. \(\forall x \exists y(x + y = 5)\)

In the first statement we say that there is a number which is always equal to 5-x no matter which x (this is false)

In the second statemnet we say that for all numbers x there is a y such that x + y = 5 (which is true)

Quantifier Negation laws:

  • \(\lnot \exists x P(x) \iff \forall x \lnot P(x)\)
  • \(\forall x P(x) \iff \exists x \lnot P(x)\)

Quantifier Distributive laws:

  • \( \forall x(E(x) \land T (x)) \iff \forall xE(x) \land \forall xT(x)\)
  • \( \forall x(E(x) \lor T (x)) \iff \forall xE(x) \lor \forall xT(x)\)
  • \(\exists x(E(x) \lor T (x)) \iff \exists xE(x) \lor \exists xT(x)\)
The phrase Exactly once occurs so often in mathematics that there is a special notation for it.

\(\exists! x P(x)\)
It is a remarkable fact that the structure of all mathematical statements can be understood using these symbols, and all mathematical reasoning can be analyzed in terms of the proper use of these symbols.

2. Use proof by contradiction; that is, assume that P is true and try to reach a contradiction.

1. Reexpress as a positive statement.
2. In a proof by contradiction, you can reach a contradiction by proving P.
\(P \Rightarrow Q\)
1. Assume P is true and prove Q.
2. Prove the contrapositive; that is, assume that Q is false and prove that P is false.
\(P \Rightarrow Q\)
1. If you are also given P, or you can prove that P is true, then you can conclude that Q is true.
2. Use the contrapositive.
\(P \land Q\)
Prove P and Q separately. In other words, treat this as two separate goals: P, and Q.
\(P \land Q\)
Treat this as two givens: P, and Q.
\(P \lor Q\)
1. Assume P is false and prove Q, or assume Q is false and prove P.
2. Use proof by cases. In each case, either prove P or prove Q.
\(P \lor Q\)
1. Use proof by cases. In case 1 assume that P is true, and in case 2 assume that Q is true.
2. If you are also given that P is false, or you can prove that P is false, then you can conclude that Q is true and vice versa.
